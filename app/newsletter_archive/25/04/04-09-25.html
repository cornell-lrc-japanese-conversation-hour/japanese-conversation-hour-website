不登校とひきこもり: School Avoidance in Japan
Tami Takada

If you keep up with Japanese news, you’ve probably encountered the so-called ひきこもり問題—literally, “the social reclusion problem.” The term “ひきこもり” has become part of the Japanese vernacular, with ひきこもり making appearances in popular novels and anime in addition to news reporting.

In 2022, it was estimated that about 2% of the Japanese working-age population qualified as ひきこもり (hikikomori), or people who mostly stay at home, avoiding school, work, and social activities. About 20% of ひきこもり are thought to derive directly from 不登校（ふとうこう）, or school avoidance, which was found to affect about 5% of all K-12 students in Japan in 2023 (文部科学省). The below results from a 2024 study conducted by KHJ (全国ひきこもり家族会連合会）suggest that adverse school experiences have long reaching effects, so much so that the top two reasons cited for becoming a ひきこもり were failed friendships/relationships at school and school bullying/violence.

The Japanese government conducts annual studies on the state of loneliness in the Japanese population and “problematic behaviors” in the school-age population, analyzing factors like social withdrawal and school bullying. Yet despite the lack of comparative coverage these issues seem to get in America, they’re not uniquely Japanese phenomena. 27.8% of K-12 students in America were chronically absent in 2022, and school avoidance has been on the rise since the pandemic.
