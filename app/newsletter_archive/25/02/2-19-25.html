僕と私と我輩 | First Person Pronouns in Japanese
Tami Takada

私（わたし） Watashi is one of, if not the most commonly used first person pronoun in Japanese. It is commonly used in formal settings such as conversations with one’s superior or a teacher, as well as in informal settings as a primarily female pronoun. わたし is sometimes further informalized to あたし (atashi) to sound more feminine.

僕（ぼく） Boku is the second most popular choice for primarily male speakers in formal settings. While non-native speakers tend to use 私 for formal conversations, native speakers often use 僕 even in conversations with superiors. 僕 is also popular in informal settings but lags behind 俺, perhaps due to its more polite or round image.

俺（おれ） Ore is the most popular informal pronoun for primarily male speakers, but would be considered rude in formal settings and even in the context of first-time meetings or introductions to peers.

うち Uchi is a gender-neutral first person pronoun used in informal settings. According to one study, うち was considered the “coolest” first person pronoun after 俺. 自分 (jibun) is another popular gender-neutral first person pronoun, but is more formal than うち (it was also considered less cool).

わし Washi is a primarily male first person pronoun used mostly by elderly men. It appears to be more popular in Western rather than Eastern/Northern Japan.

名前 Younger children often refer to themselves with their own name, sometimes even adding ちゃん or くん to the ends of their names. However, it is typically considered impolite to refer to oneself with a suffix, even an informal one like ちゃん or くん, and bizarre for an adult to refer to themselves by their own name.
