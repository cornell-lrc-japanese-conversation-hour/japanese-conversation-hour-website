語呂合わせで勉強しましょう！
Cougar Hashikura

語呂合わせ（ごろあわせ）Goroawase refers to a type of Japanese wordplay in which correspondences are made between numbers and the ways in which they are pronounced. Since each of the 漢数字（かんすうじ）have a native Japanese 訓読み（くんよみ）reading, a Chinese 音読み（おんよみ）reading, and an English-based reading, there are often ways to combine different readings for different numbers to turn long sequences of seemingly random numbers into an easy mnemonic to remember. Many students in Japan do this as a way to remember dates, values, and phone numbers!

A popular example of 語呂合わせ is a method to remember the year 1492, which was when Columbus first travelled to America. One, which can be read as いち, is shortened to just い; four, as よん, is shortened to just よ; nine is normally read as きゅう, but the alternative 音読み reading く is used instead; and two is kept the same, as に. Combining these readings, 1492 can be read as 「いよくに」, which can be interpreted as 「いいよ、国」or “It’s a good country.” Alternatively, you might see it be extended into 「いいよ、国が見えた！」, or “Alright, I can see land!” mimicking what Columbus himself might have said when he reached America.

Some other examples include:

  • To remember the first nine digits of pi, 3.14159265, some students use the 語呂合わせ「産医師異国に向こう」（さんいしいこくにむこう）which means “An obstetrician faces towards a foreign nation.” The phrase is split into the phonemes さん|い|し|い|こ|く|に|む|こう, with the quirk that five is read as こ and こう. Despite not being actual readings for the character 五, 語呂合わせ allows for some level of flexibility.
  • On a darker side, to remember the length of a sidereal day, which is 23 hours, 56 minutes and 4 seconds, the 語呂合わせ「兄さん殺し」（にいさんごろし）meaning “Killing one’s brother” might be used. The phrase is split into the phonemes にい|さん|ご|ろ|し.

Social media has also done the work of making 語呂合わせ in the opposite direction, where numbers are aligned with common phrases to shorten them for messages. One very popular example is 39, which when read digit-by-digit, can be pronounced as 「さんきゅう」sounding much like the English “Thank you.” Likewise, the number 4649 can be read as 「よろしく」or “Best regards.”
