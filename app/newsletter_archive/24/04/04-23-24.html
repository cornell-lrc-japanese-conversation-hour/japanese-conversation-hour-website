BREAKING NEWS

Tami Takada
School avoidance rates in Tokushima Prefecture have reached an all time high this year, with almost 2000 elementary and middle school students reportedly refusing to attend school. This year, the Ministry of Education changed its policy from bringing students back to schools to considering alternative learning environments such as “free schools” instead. The number of free schools, or locally organized community centers for children, have been increasing over the years. Now with government backing, there is hope that they will be able to provide better support to all students who are unable to attend school.

頭が上がらない vs. 頭が下がる

Bora Shin
There are various idioms related to body parts in Japanese. Among them, do you know the difference between "頭が上がらない" (atama-ga-agaranai) and "頭が下がる" (atama-ga-sagaru)? When translated word-for-word to English, these two expressions have very similar meanings—respectively, "not being able to lift one's head" and "lowering one's head." However, these idioms have different meanings.

To begin with, "頭が上がらない" refers to a situation were one "is no match for another" or is "indebted to another." The expression is often used in a context where one feels a great discrepancy in ability or feels sorry due to their actions.

On the other hand, "頭が下がる" means "to feel admiration and respect from the bottom of one's heart (行いが立派で心の底から感心して尊敬すること)."

EXAMPLES
「自分のミスで取引先（とりひきさき）から契約（けいやく）を切られそうだった時、一緒に謝罪（しゃざい）して助けてくれた上司には頭が上がらない」
I feel deeply indebted to my boss who apologized with me when I was on the brink of losing a contract with a client due to my own mistake
「試験中なのに私の勉強を見てくれて本当に頭が下がる」
I truly respect (and am grateful to) you for helping me with my studies even during the exam period
