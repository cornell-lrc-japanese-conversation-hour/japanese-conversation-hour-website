SNIPPET OF HISTORY

Tami Takada
In 2022, NHK released one of its most popular Taiga series of all time, The 13 Lords of the Shogun (鎌倉殿の13人). Lords centers on historical figure Hojo Yoshitoki (北条義時), who assisted Minamoto no Yoritomo (源頼朝) in his rise to his position as shogun and kamakura-dono following the defeat of the rival Taira clan in 1185 (治承・寿永の乱). The Minamoto line died out after just three generations, but Yoshitoki began an almost century-long dominance of the Hojo clan (known as 得宗専制政治) as the true political leaders behind the shogun (執権). Hojo dominance crumbled following the Mongol attacks in the late 13th century and Emperor Go-daigo’s coup d'etat.

Source: Haruo Sasayama, et al., Shōsetsu Nihonshi Kaiteiban (Tokyo: Yamakawa Shuppansha, 2022), 96-121.

Sara Goto
[ɕäbä]—referring to the Sahā world in Mahāyāna Buddhism, means the mundane world or regular society; also means the free world outside of military or prison.

聞く vs. 聴く

Bora Shin
Although they are both read as “きく[kiku]” and have meaning that has to do with hearing, “聞く” and “聴く” have different nuances. “聞く[kiku]” refers to naturally hearing something; that is, hearing something without paying dedicated attention to whatever auditory stimuli that is (自然に耳に入る). For instance, you could say that you heard(聞いた[kiita]) a rumor(うわさ) or an announcement(アナウンス). On the other hand, “聴く[kiku]” refers to carefully listening or consciously giving ear to something (注意深く耳を傾ける). You could use this expression for music (音楽), lectures(講義), or other circumstances (事情).

EXAMPLES
「物音を聞いたので駆（か）けつけてみた」
I rushed there since I heard a noise
「講義を聴きながらメモを取った」
I wrote notes while taking the lecture
