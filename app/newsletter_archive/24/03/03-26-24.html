SNIPPET OF HISTORY

Tami Takada
In 1853, Commodore Matthew Perry led four battleships to Uraga to deliver a list of demands from President Fillmore to the Japanese government. Fillmore expressed a desire to open trade relations between Japan and the US, and was not above using military might to make Japan bend. Outside of highly limited relations with a handful of countries, Edo period Japan had mostly been closed off to the world (鎖国状態（さこくじょうたい）). Perry’s arrival, known as 黒船来航 (くろふねらいこう) in Japan, triggered a crisis within the Tokugawa military government, which struggled to globalize amidst intimidation from the West, sudden inflation due to engaging in the global silk trade, and unrest from xenophobic and anti-government groups.

Source: Satoru Fujita, Bakumatsu kara ishin e (Tokyo: Iwanami Shoten, 2022).

WEEKLY VOCAB

Sara Goto
[no̞be̞t͡sɯ̟ mäkɯ̟näɕi]—a saying used to describe something that goes on and on, like an act that continues forever (のべつ) without (無し) pulling the curtains (幕).

EXAMPLES
「あいつのべつ幕無しにしゃべるから早く切り上げといて」
Bro keeps yapping forever so be sure to cut it short
「のべつ幕無しにインスタのリール見てたら夜が明けてた」
Was endlessly scrolling through insta reels and before I knew it the sun was up

LOOK OUT

Bora Shin
When we want to address a person who knowingly commits a wrongful act (悪いことであるとわかっていながら故意（こい）に行（おこな）う人), we often use the term "確信犯（かくしんはん） [kakushin-han]." However, this is a misappropriation of the term, as "確信犯" actually refers to an individual who commits a crime based on their firm belief or conviction, or to the act itself (信念（しんねん）に基（もと）づいて正しいと信じてなされる犯罪行為（はんざいこうい）やその行為（こうい）を行った犯罪者（はんざいしゃ）).

EXAMPLES
「政治家を殺した彼女は確信犯だ」
The politician’s murderer was confirmed to be a terrorist.
「先日起こった犯罪は、確信犯だったと噂されている」
The crime that occurred the other day is rumored to have been committed by someone acting with firm conviction.
