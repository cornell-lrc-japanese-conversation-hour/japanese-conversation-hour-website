新書 Culture in Japan
Tami Takada

Alongside aisles of contemporary fiction, newly released hardcovers, and bookstore recommendations, modern Japanese bookstores always contain a section dedicated to 新書 (しんしょ, shinsho). 新書 refer to any books that share a specific set of relatively tall and narrow dimensions (173 by 105mm), but have come to encompass a unique genre of lightweight nonfiction offering an overview of scholarly work for the layperson.

岩波書店 (Iwanami Publishing) is often credited with beginning the 新書 trend in the early Showa era, and their 岩波新書 series continues to this day. In the 50s, other publishers began creating their own 新書 series, leading to a 新書 boom. Today, 岩波新書, 中公新書 (Chuko shinsho), and 講談社現代新書 (Kodansha Gendai shinsho) are known as the “新書の御三家（ごさんけ）,” or the “Big 3” of 新書.

新書 have had a profound impact on Japanese culture. Famous works like 丸山真男 (Maruyama Masao)’s “日本の思想” (“Japanese Philosophy”) and 木村敏 (Kimura Bin)’s “時間と自己” (“Time and the Self”) continue to be regarded as classics representing Japanese thought, while other works spotlight lesser known issues to bring them to the forefront of discussion in Japan, such as more recent works like 今野晴貴 (Konno Haruki)’s “ブラックバイト” (literally, “Black Part-time Work”) exposing absuive working conditions at part-time jobs, and 池上正樹 (Ikegami Masaki)’s “大人のひきこもり” (“Adult Recluses”) highlighting the lesser-known phenomenon of social reclusion amongst the older generation in Japan.
