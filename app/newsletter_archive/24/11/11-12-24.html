Japanese Traditional Desserts, 和菓子😋
Bora Shin

Do you have a sweet tooth? If so, you’re in luck! Today, we’ll be introducing you to Japanese desserts, 和菓子 (わがし, wagashi).

和菓子 refer to all different kinds of Japanese traditional sweets. They are made of plant-based ingredients–such as rice flour, red or white bean paste (餡子; anko), agar (a type of seaweed-based gelatin), and seasonal fruits–which give them a delicate sweetness, although there are many variations. Symbolic meanings are often imbued through the use of different shapes and ingredients to align with the season, natural elements, and cultural motifs, and serve a significant role in formal tea ceremonies.

和菓子 can be categorized into three types:

  1. 生菓子 (なまがし, namagashi) are fresh sweets with a moisture content above 30%. They can be further categorized into various subtypes including steamed sweets like 饅頭 (manju) and mochi-based sweets like 大福 (daifuku). Due to its moisture, 生菓子 are delicate and expire quickly (in some cases even if they are refrigerated).
  2. 半生菓子 (はんなまがし, hannamagashi) are 和菓子 with a moisture content between 10~30%. 最中 (もなかmonaka)—crisp wafers filled with a sweet filling, typically 餡子—and 羊羹(ようかん, youkan)—firm, jelly-like sweets made from red bean paste, sugar, and agar, often sliced and served in rectangular blocks—are well-known 半生菓子.
  3. 干菓子 (ひがし, higashi) are 和菓子 with a moisture content below 10%, making them the most long-lasting among the three. One example of 干菓子 is かりんとう (karintou)—snacks often made of deep-fried dough, typically crafted from wheat flour and yeast, then coated in a sugar glaze.
