A Shocking Twist in Japan's House of Representatives Election
Tami Takada

Last month, Japanese voters overturned the 自民党 (Liberal Democratic Party)'s majority in the 衆議院（しゅうぎいん, lower house of the Diet, House of Representatives), which it had held almost continuously since 1955. The right wing 自民党and its coalition partner 公明党 took 215 seats, short of a 233 majority, while the main opposition party, the liberal leaning 立憲民主党 (りっけんみんしゅとう, Constitutional Democratic Party, abbrev. 立民党), took 148 seats.

Voters have long been discontented with the state of the Japanese economy, including static wages and inflation. Combined with the embezzlement scandal that forced former 自民党 prime minister Kishida to resign, the 自民党 lost significant voter support.

Current 自民党 prime minister Ishiba Shigeru (石破茂) has indicated surprise over the results, but vowed to continue internal party reforms to regain voters' trust.
