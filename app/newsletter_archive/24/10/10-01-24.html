秋は夕暮れ 🍂 A Glimpse of Classical Japanese
Tami Takada

Although grammar is no longer a common subject in American schools, it's still key to one part of the Japanese high school curriculum: 古典文法（こてんぶんぽう）, or 古文. 古文 encompasses the study of classical Japanese language and literature extending back to the Heian era, usually involving texts like 源氏物語 (Tale of Genji), 徒然草（つれづれぐさ, Essays in Idleness）, 枕草子 (まくらのそうし, The Pillow Book), and 古今和歌集 (こきんわかしゅう, “Collection of Japanese Poems of Ancient and Modern Times”).

春すきて　夏来（き）にけらし　しろたへの　衣（ころも）ほすてふ　天（あま）のかくやま
持統（じとう）天皇’s poem featured in「百人一首」

Peter MacMillan's translation: Spring has passed, and the white robes of summer are being aired on fragrant Mount Kagu—beloved of the gods.

Texts prior to the end of World War II used 歴史的仮名遣（かなづか）い, or historical lettering, which doesn’t always align with modern pronunciations. The above poem features three quirks of this older かな usage:

  1. しろたへ (white robes) is pronounced in modern Japanese as しろたえ
    When ひふへほ are used anywhere in a word except at the very beginning, they are usually pronounced in modern Japanese as いうえお.
  2. ほすてふ (airing/drying) becomes ほすちょう
    Small letters like 「っ」and「ょ」were not part of 歴史的仮名遣い, so combinations like てふ、せふ、and けふ are substituted in modern Japanese with ちょう、しょう、and きょう.
  3. かくやま (Mount Kagu) and すきて (passed) both have implied 濁点（だくてん） that make them かぐやま and すぎて
    濁点 notation was only standardized in the 16th century, so when reading historical texts, you have to know which words carry implicit 濁点 (voiced consonants).

Curious to learn more? Cornell’s Professor John Whitman teaches LING/ASIAN 4411: “The History of the Japanese Language,” which explores the evolution of the Japanese language over time.
